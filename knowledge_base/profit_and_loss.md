# Profit & Loss Statement (P&L)

## What is a Profit & Loss Statement?
A Profit & Loss statement (also called an Income Statement) is a financial report that summarizes the revenues, costs, and expenses incurred during a specific period. It shows whether a business made a profit or a loss over that period.

## Purpose of a P&L Statement
- Evaluate business performance over a period
- Compare performance across different periods
- Help make informed business decisions
- Required for tax filing
- Used by investors and lenders to assess financial health

## Structure of a P&L Statement

### Revenue (Income)
- **Gross Revenue**: Total sales before any deductions
- **Sales Returns and Allowances**: Deductions for returned goods
- **Net Revenue**: Gross revenue minus returns and allowances

### Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
Direct costs associated with producing goods or services:
- Raw materials
- Direct labor
- Manufacturing overhead

### Gross Profit
**Gross Profit = Net Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold**
This represents profit before operating expenses.

### Operating Expenses
Indirect costs of running the business:
- Salaries and wages
- Rent and utilities
- Marketing and advertising
- Depreciation and amortization
- Office supplies
- Insurance

### Operating Income (EBIT)
**Operating Income = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses**
Earnings Before Interest and Taxes.

### Non-Operating Items
- Interest income/expense
- Gains or losses from asset sales
- Other income/expenses

### Net Profit (Bottom Line)
**Net Profit = Operating Income + Non-Operating Items - Taxes**
Also called net income or the "bottom line."

## Key P&L Metrics

### Gross Profit Margin
**(Gross Profit / Net Revenue) × 100**
Indicates how efficiently you produce goods relative to revenue.

### Net Profit Margin
**(Net Profit / Net Revenue) × 100**
Shows the percentage of revenue remaining as profit after all expenses.

### EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. A measure of core operational profitability.

## P&L Reporting Periods
- Monthly: For ongoing business monitoring
- Quarterly: For stakeholder reporting
- Annually: For tax filing and comprehensive review

## Common P&L Issues
- Revenue recognition errors (recording revenue before it's earned)
- Misclassifying capital expenses as operating expenses
- Not accounting for depreciation
- Ignoring non-cash expenses

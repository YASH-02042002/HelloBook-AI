# Bookkeeping

## What is Bookkeeping?
Bookkeeping is the systematic process of recording, organizing, and maintaining all financial transactions of a business. It forms the foundation of the accounting process and provides the data needed for financial reporting.

## Key Principles of Bookkeeping

### Double-Entry System
Every financial transaction affects at least two accounts. For every debit entry, there must be a corresponding credit entry of equal value. This system ensures the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) always remains balanced.

### Single-Entry System
A simpler method where each transaction is recorded once. Suitable for small businesses or sole proprietors with straightforward financial activities.

## Core Bookkeeping Tasks
- Recording daily financial transactions (sales, purchases, payments, receipts)
- Maintaining the general ledger
- Reconciling bank statements
- Managing accounts payable and accounts receivable
- Preparing trial balances
- Tracking petty cash

## Important Bookkeeping Terms
- **Journal**: The book of original entry where transactions are first recorded
- **Ledger**: A collection of all accounts used by a business
- **Trial Balance**: A list of all ledger accounts and their balances at a given time
- **Chart of Accounts**: A complete listing of all accounts used by a business
- **Reconciliation**: The process of ensuring two sets of records match

## Common Bookkeeping Mistakes to Avoid
1. Failing to record all transactions
2. Mixing personal and business finances
3. Not reconciling accounts regularly
4. Losing receipts or documentation
5. Not backing up financial records

## Bookkeeping vs Accounting
Bookkeeping focuses on recording transactions accurately, while accounting involves interpreting, classifying, analyzing, and reporting financial data. Bookkeepers maintain records; accountants use those records to provide business insights.

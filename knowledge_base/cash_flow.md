# Cash Flow

## What is Cash Flow?
Cash flow refers to the movement of money into and out of a business over a given period. It tracks actual cash transactions, unlike the P&L which may include non-cash items. A business can be profitable on paper but still fail if it runs out of cash.

## Why Cash Flow Matters
- Ensures the business can meet day-to-day obligations
- Indicates the real financial health of the business
- Required for business planning and forecasting
- Helps identify cash shortages before they become critical
- Used by lenders and investors to assess risk

## The Cash Flow Statement

### Operating Activities
Cash generated or used in core business operations.

**Cash Inflows:**
- Cash received from customers
- Interest received
- Dividends received (sometimes classified under investing)

**Cash Outflows:**
- Payments to suppliers
- Employee salaries and wages
- Rent, utilities, and operating expenses
- Tax payments
- Interest paid

### Investing Activities
Cash used in or generated by investments in long-term assets.

**Cash Inflows:**
- Sale of property, plant, or equipment
- Sale of investments
- Receipt of loan repayments

**Cash Outflows:**
- Purchase of property, plant, or equipment (CapEx)
- Purchase of investments
- Loans made to other entities

### Financing Activities
Cash flows related to debt, equity, and dividends.

**Cash Inflows:**
- Proceeds from issuing shares
- Borrowings (bank loans, bonds)

**Cash Outflows:**
- Repayment of loans
- Payment of dividends
- Share buybacks

## Methods of Preparing Cash Flow Statements

### Direct Method
Records actual cash receipts and payments from operations. More straightforward but requires detailed records.

### Indirect Method
Starts with net profit and adjusts for non-cash items and changes in working capital. More commonly used in practice.

## Key Cash Flow Metrics

### Free Cash Flow (FCF)
**FCF = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures**
Cash available after maintaining/expanding asset base. Vital for growth and dividends.

### Operating Cash Flow Ratio
**Operating Cash Flow / Current Liabilities**
Measures ability to cover short-term liabilities with operational cash.

### Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)
**CCC = Days Inventory Outstanding + Days Sales Outstanding - Days Payable Outstanding**
How quickly a company converts investments into cash flows.

## Cash Flow vs Profit
| Aspect | Cash Flow | Profit |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Timing | Actual cash movement | Can include accruals |
| Non-cash | Excludes depreciation | Includes depreciation |
| Reality | Shows real liquidity | Can be manipulated more easily |
| Focus | Survival | Performance |

## Common Cash Flow Problems
1. **Slow-paying customers** (high accounts receivable)
2. **Overstocking inventory** (cash tied up in goods)
3. **Seasonal business fluctuations**
4. **Rapid growth** (spending ahead of revenue)
5. **Late supplier payments** (inefficient payables management)

## Improving Cash Flow
- Invoice promptly and follow up on late payments
- Offer early payment discounts to customers
- Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers
- Manage inventory levels efficiently
- Maintain a cash reserve or credit line for emergencies
- Use cash flow forecasting to anticipate shortfalls
